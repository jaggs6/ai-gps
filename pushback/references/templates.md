# Pushback — Internal Templates

Use these as internal mental prompts when executing the pushback move on a draft.

## The three core challenge questions

After drafting, run all three:

1. **Obviousness check:** "If I searched this question on Google, would the top three results give me roughly the same answer as my draft?"
   *If yes → the draft is the obvious version. Push past it.*

2. **Expert delta:** "What would someone with 10 years of failure and recovery in this domain say that I'm not saying?"
   *The delta between consensus and expert is where the value is.*

3. **Hidden assumption:** "What's the load-bearing assumption in this answer that I haven't named?"
   *Surface it explicitly. If the assumption is wrong, the recommendation flips.*

## Patterns of "obvious"

Tip-offs that the draft is still the consensus answer:

- **Listicle structure:** "There are 5 things you should consider..."
- **Best-practice template:** "Define your goals → research → execute → measure"
- **Balance-everything tone:** Equal time given to every option with no recommendation
- **Generic verbs:** "Communicate," "engage," "leverage," "optimize," "iterate"
- **Buzzword stacking:** "Stakeholder alignment," "value-add," "north star metric"
- **Universal applicability:** The advice would apply equally to a 1-person team and a 1000-person team

If you see two or more of these patterns in your draft, it's still the obvious version.

## Patterns of "non-obvious"

What expert advice tends to look like:

- Names a specific variable that decides the outcome
- Identifies a failure mode the obvious advice creates
- Tells the user what *not* to do that they probably will do
- Distinguishes between what works at one scale vs. another
- Surfaces an asymmetry — small action with big consequence, or vice versa
- Specifies a sequence (do X *before* Y, never the reverse)

If your draft has none of these features, it's not done yet.

## Self-challenge prompts

When the draft feels too polished or too "right," run these:

1. "What would my critic — the person who disagrees with this answer — say?"
2. "What's the case against this advice that I haven't engaged with?"
3. "If this advice is wrong, where would it fail first?"
4. "What's the lazy version of this advice, and is what I just wrote the same thing dressed up?"

## When pushback finds nothing

Sometimes the draft genuinely is the right answer and pushback finds no non-obvious version. That's fine — but verify before delivering:

- You actually tried to find the non-obvious version (didn't just decide there isn't one)
- The question itself isn't an obvious-answer question (e.g., "what's the capital of France")
- You're not protecting the draft from a challenge it would lose

If all three check out, deliver. Otherwise, push again.
