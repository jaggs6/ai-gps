# Pushback — Calibration Examples

Before/after examples showing what changes when you challenge your own answer. The "before" here is already better than a generic default — pushback finds what's still missing. If your output still looks like the "before" column, find the assumption you haven't named yet.

---

## Example: "Should I raise my SaaS prices by 30%?"

*(Continuing from a gaslit draft that already recommends repackaging before raising prices)*

**Before (post-gaslight, pre-pushback):**

> Don't raise prices uniformly. Repackage the offer as an upgrade rather than a tax. Lead with your top revenue tier, tier the offering, and sequence the rollout by account size.

**After (pushback applied):**

> Don't raise prices uniformly — but also, "raising prices" might be the wrong frame entirely. The founders who do this well don't announce a price increase. They introduce a new tier that happens to be better than what the customer has now, price it at 30% more, and let the customer choose to move up. The increase is invisible because it's attached to something new.
>
> The assumption in the "repackage" advice is that you have something meaningful to add to justify the new tier. If you don't — if your product hasn't changed — customers will see through it immediately and the framing makes you look worse, not better. So the real question before pricing is: what changed that earns the increase?

**What changed:** The gaslit version gave the right move (repackage). Pushback found the assumption buried in it (you have something new to add) and named what happens when that assumption is false.

## Example: "How do I grow my LinkedIn following as a B2B founder?"

**Before (post-gaslight, pre-pushback):**

> Post consistently, use strong hooks, write about what you know, engage in comments, and don't sell in the first line. Volume and consistency compound over 3-6 months.

**After (pushback applied):**

> The consistency advice is right but most people apply it wrong — they post into the void and wait for the algorithm to find them. What actually works in the first 90 days is distribution, not content. Find 5-10 accounts with your exact audience who are growing, engage genuinely and specifically on their posts for 3 weeks before your content ramps up, and borrow their distribution before you build your own.
>
> The wrong assumption in "post consistently" is that LinkedIn surfaces your content to strangers. It doesn't — it surfaces it to people who already engage with you. If that pool is zero, posting is a tree falling in an empty forest. Build the engagement network first, then fill it with content.

**What changed:** Pushback found the assumption in the generic advice (LinkedIn shows your content to strangers) and replaced it with the non-obvious mechanism (build a distribution network before the content).
