# Gaslight — Calibration Examples

Before/after examples showing what changes when you raise the stakes on a draft answer. If your output still looks like the "before" column, push harder.

---

## Example: "Should I raise my SaaS prices by 30%?"

**Before (default draft):**

> Raising prices is a balance between revenue and retention. Consider segmenting your customers, grandfathering existing accounts, communicating the change clearly, and testing before rolling out broadly. The right call depends on your market and customer base.

**After (gaslight applied):**

> Don't raise prices uniformly. The real question isn't whether to charge 30% more — it's whether you can repackage what you offer so the increase looks like an upgrade rather than a tax.
>
> Lead with your top revenue tier. If 20% of customers drive 80% of revenue, they don't get a price increase — they get a value expansion conversation first. Then tier the offering so the increase becomes a choice they make (which tier do I want?), not a fact they react to. Run the change on your smallest accounts first — churn there is signal, not catastrophe.
>
> That's the order: repackage → tier → sequence by account size.

**What changed:** The default listed options with no guidance. The gaslit version takes a position ("don't raise uniformly"), gives a specific sequence, and names the mechanism (repackage as upgrade, not tax).

## Example: "How do I get my team to actually use the new process?"

**Before (default draft):**

> Getting team adoption requires clear communication, training, and buy-in from leadership. Make sure the process is well-documented, explain the benefits, involve team members in the rollout, and follow up to reinforce the behaviour over time.

**After (gaslight applied):**

> The documentation isn't the problem. The problem is that new processes ask people to be slower in the short term, and nobody has made it safe to be slow.
>
> Two things work: (1) find the one person who's closest to neutral on the change — not the champion, not the resistor — and make their adoption visible. Resistors watch the middle, not the champion. (2) Remove the old process entirely instead of running both in parallel. As long as the old way works, the new way is optional.

**What changed:** The default gave a checklist. The gaslit version identifies the actual mechanism (safety to be slow, social proof from the middle, removing optionality) and drops the checklist framing.
