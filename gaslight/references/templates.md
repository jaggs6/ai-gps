# Gaslight — Internal Templates

Use these as internal mental prompts when executing the gaslight move. Pick the archetype that matches the user's domain, then run the self-prompts against your draft.

## Archetypes by domain

The "hard-to-impress expert" you imagine reading your draft should match the user's question domain.

| Domain | Archetype | Triggers on questions like |
|--------|-----------|----------------------------|
| Business strategy | A CEO who's run two profitable companies and one that failed | "should I pivot," "should I expand," strategic decisions |
| Pricing / monetization | A founder who's had pricing power and lost it | pricing, packaging, monetization questions |
| Hiring / team | A manager who's hired 50 people and fired 15 | hiring, firing, management questions |
| Product | A PM who's killed three of their own features | product, feature, prioritization questions |
| Engineering | A staff engineer who's debugged the same bug at three companies | architecture, refactoring, technical decisions |
| Sales | A rep who's lost a 7-figure deal in the final week | sales, negotiation, deal questions |
| Marketing / growth | A marketer who's burned $500k on a channel that didn't work | growth, channels, marketing questions |
| Career | A senior who took a "great offer" and regretted it | career change, job offers, promotion questions |
| Investing | A VC who's seen the same pitch deck three times | startup decisions, fundraising, investment questions |
| Health / fitness | A coach who's seen every motivation collapse | habit, health, fitness, longevity questions |
| Personal finance | A planner who's watched clients chase yield | savings, investing, retirement questions |
| Writing / content | An editor who's killed 200 of someone else's drafts | writing, content, communication questions |

If the user's domain isn't listed: pick the role most likely to have personally failed at this exact decision before.

## Self-prompts

Run these against the draft:

1. "If [archetype] were reading this over my shoulder, what would they immediately dismiss?"
2. "What's in this draft that sounds smart but doesn't actually commit to anything?"
3. "Where am I saying 'it depends' without saying what it depends on?"
4. "What hedge is in this answer that an expert would cut?"

## Replacement patterns

When you catch the patterns on the left, replace with the patterns on the right:

| Generic pattern | Sharp replacement |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| "It depends on your situation" | "It depends on [specific variable]" |
| "Consider [list of factors]" | "The factor that decides this is [X]" |
| "You should think about both pros and cons" | "Here's the position. Here's what would change my mind" |
| "There's no one-size-fits-all answer" | "For your situation specifically, the answer is [X]" |
| "Many experts recommend..." | "The actual mechanism is..." |
| "Communicate clearly" / "Be transparent" | A specific script of what to say |
| "Test before rolling out" | A specific test design with success criteria |

## Stakes framing

Internally, name the stakes before you draft. The model produces sharper output when stakes are present in its working context — this is the mechanism gaslight exploits.

- "If this advice is wrong, the user loses [concrete consequence]"
- "If this is generic and the user knows it, they'll never trust this answer source again"
- "The reader is someone who's already been burned by the obvious version of this advice"

Hold the consequence in mind while drafting. The answer will change.
