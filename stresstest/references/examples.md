# Stress Test — Calibration Examples

Before/after examples showing what the three stress test checks catch. The "before" here is already sharp — the stress test finds what's still incomplete, biased, or underqualified.

---

## Example: "Should I raise my SaaS prices by 30%?"

*(Continuing from a draft that already recommends repackaging into a new tier)*

**Before (post-gaslight and pushback, pre-stress test):**

> Don't raise prices — introduce a new tier priced 30% higher than your current top plan. Let customers opt up rather than absorbing a price increase on what they already have. Build the tier around something that changed (faster delivery, priority support, senior access). Run it on new customers first, then invite existing customers to move up.

**After (stress test applied):**

> Same recommendation — but three things the draft didn't account for:
>
> **Gap check surfaced:** The advice assumes you have something new to bundle into the tier. If your product hasn't meaningfully changed in the last 12 months, the tier feels manufactured and customers will say so publicly. Before building the tier, answer: what's the one thing your best customers wish they had more of? That's the anchor.
>
> **Bias sweep caught:** Survivorship bias. This tier strategy is modeled on SaaS companies that had strong net revenue retention before the increase. It works well when existing customers are growing with you; it's much riskier when you have high annual churn. If your gross churn is above 15%, stabilise that first.
>
> **Stakes injected:** If you introduce the tier and your top 3 accounts don't move up — or worse, use it as a reason to re-evaluate the contract — you've signalled that the old tier is being deprioritised without delivering the value of the new one. Before launch, have a direct conversation with your top 3 accounts. Not to sell them. To ask: "We're thinking about adding X. Would that change how you use us?"
>
> Three things would sharpen this further:
> 1. What's your current gross churn rate, and how does it vary across account size?
> 2. What has meaningfully changed in your product in the last 12 months?
> 3. Are your top 3 accounts on annual contracts, and when do they renew?

**What changed:** The stress test found the survivorship bias (works better with low churn), surfaced the gap (what actually earns the new tier), reframed the stakes (top accounts using this as a re-evaluation trigger), and ended with three questions that would unlock a sharper recommendation.

## Example: "Should I hire a full-time editor for my YouTube channel?"

**Before (pre-stress test):**

> At 500k subscribers posting 3-4 videos/week, you're at the scale where a full-time editor makes sense. The editing bottleneck is real, and a consistent hire gives you more reliability than freelancers. Factor in onboarding time and get someone aligned with your style.

**After (stress test applied):**

> Same direction, but three things to check first:
>
> **Gap check surfaced:** "Editing bottleneck" could mean two different things — you're spending too many hours editing (a time problem), or your videos aren't performing as well as they should (a quality problem). A full-time hire solves the time problem. If it's a quality problem, hiring might make things worse — you'd ship more videos with the same retention issue.
>
> **Bias sweep caught:** Confirmation bias. You framed this as an editing bottleneck and I optimised around it. But at 100k views/video with 500k subscribers, that's a 20% reach rate — which might mean packaging or hook issues upstream of the edit. Worth checking retention curves before assuming the editor is the lever.
>
> **Stakes injected:** If you hire full-time and it doesn't move the needle in 90 days, you've added $60-80k in annual cost and a management obligation right when you should be scaling. The lower-risk path: hire a freelancer for 2 videos this month and 2 videos using your current process. Compare CTR, 30-second retention, and hours saved. That's a $500-1000 experiment that answers the same question.
>
> Three things would sharpen this:
> 1. What does your average retention curve look like — specifically the drop-off in the first 60 seconds?
> 2. How many hours per video are you personally spending on editing right now?
> 3. Have you worked with freelance editors before, and what broke down?

**What changed:** The stress test caught the confirmation bias (assuming editing is the real bottleneck), surfaced the gap (time problem vs. quality problem), reframed the stakes (90-day cost of being wrong), and offered a cheap experiment instead.
